Phensang Monastery

Phensang Monastery, also known as Phensang Sangag Choding Monastery, is a prominent Buddhist monastery located in North Sikkim. It is renowned for its picturesque location and its significant role in the Nyingmapa Buddhist order

History & Significance

Foundation:The monastery was founded in 1721 by the third Lhatsun Jigmed Pawo. It was originally built to serve as a residence for three monks, but it was expanded in 1840 to accommodate a growing number of monks.

Reconstruction: The monastery has faced several calamities. In 1947, a fire completely destroyed the original structure. It was rebuilt in 1948 through the efforts of the lamas. Unfortunately, it was again badly damaged by heavy rain in 1983 and had to be completely rebuilt from 1985 to 1996 with government assistance

The Nyingmapa Order: Phensang is an important monastery of the Nyingmapa sect, the oldest of the four major schools of Tibetan Buddhism. It is one of the six largest monasteries in Sikkim and houses a large number of monks

Festivals, Events, and Rituals

Ralang Monastery is a vibrant hub of monastic activity, particularly during its annual festivals. These events are a spectacular display of cultural and religious traditions.

Losoong Festival

While the main Chaam dance happens before Losoong, the monastery and its monks play a central role in the larger community celebrations for the new year. Losoong is a harvest festival celebrated by the Bhutia and Lepcha communities.

  • Symbolism of Renewal: Losoong symbolizes the purging of negativity and evil spirits from the past year. It is a time for people to reflect, cleanse their surroundings, and welcome positive energies for the year ahead.
  • Cultural Adaptation: The festival is adapted from the customs and rituals of the Tibetan New Year, Losar. Over time, it has evolved to incorporate a unique blend of indigenous Lepcha and Bhutia traditions with Buddhist practices.

Kagyed Chaam (Masked Dances)

Kagyed Chaam is a significant and vibrant masked dance festival celebrated in Sikkim, primarily by the Buddhist communities. It is a powerful spiritual and cultural event that symbolizes the triumph of good over evil and marks the transition from the old year to the new.

  • Symbolism: The term "Kagyed" is of Bhutia origin and refers to the "oral transmission of the eight tantric deities." The dance is performed to honor these deities and invoke their blessings to protect humanity from malevolent spirits, diseases, and calamities
  • Purification: The festival is essentially a rite of purification. It aims to destroy all the negative forces and obstacles that have accumulated during the year, paving the way for peace, prosperity, and good fortune in the coming one

Other Important Festivals

Saga Dawa

A major Buddhist festival celebrating the birth, enlightenment, and death of the Buddha. The monastery hosts special prayers and rituals.

Lhabab Duchen

Lhabab Duchen is a major Buddhist festival that commemorates the Buddha's descent from the heavenly realms. It's celebrated on the 22nd day of the ninth Tibetan lunar month, typically in October or November.

Losar

The Tibetan New Year, celebrated in February/March with traditional rituals and gatherings.

Visiting the Monastery

Timings & Entry

  • Timings: The monastery is generally open to visitors from 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM or 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • Entry Fee: There is no entry fee, though donations are welcome.

Location & Access

  • Location:Phensang Monastery is located on a gentle slope stretching from Kabi to Phodong.
  • It is approximately 27-30 km north of Gangtok, the capital of Sikkim.
  • Transport:
    • Nearest Airport: Bagdogra Airport (IXB) in West Bengal is the closest airport.
    • Nearest Railway Station: New Jalpaiguri (NJP) in West Bengal is the closest major railway station.

Best Time to Visit

  • Spring (March-May): The weather is mild, and the surroundings are lush and green.
  • Autumn (September-November): Following the monsoon season, the skies are clear and the air is crisp, providing excellent, unobstructed views of the Himalayan mountains. This is an ideal time for sightseeing.li>
  • Winter (December-February): The weather is very cold, with temperatures often dropping to freezing point.

Tips for Visitors

  • Dress Code: Visitors are expected to dress modestly, with shoulders and knees covered.
  • Footwear: Shoes should be removed before entering the main prayer hall.
  • Etiquette: Maintain a quiet and respectful demeanor, and avoid loud conversations to honor the peaceful and spiritual environment.
  • Photography: Photography may be restricted in some areas - always ask for permission.
