Ralang Monastery

Ralang Monastery is a significant and revered Buddhist monastery located in the scenic landscapes of South Sikkim, near the town of Ravangla. It belongs to the Kagyu sect of Tibetan Buddhism and is a must-visit destination for those seeking a blend of spirituality and natural beauty.

History & Significance

Foundation: The original Ralang Monastery was built in 1768 AD by the fourth Chogyal (King) of Sikkim, Gyurmed Namgyal, to commemorate his successful pilgrimage to Tibet.

A Divine Legend: A popular legend behind the monastery's establishment is that the 9th Karmapa, the head of the Kagyu sect, threw grains of rice from his monastery in Tibet. The grains fell at the site where Ralang Monastery now stands, signifying a blessed and holy location for the new monastery.

The New Monastery: The old monastery underwent reconstruction between 1975 and 1981. A new, grander monastery, known as Palchen Choeling Monastic Institute, was constructed in 1995 by the 12th Gyaltsab Rinpoche. This new monastery is a magnificent example of authentic Tibetan architecture and is considered one of the largest monasteries in Sikkim.

Art and Artifacts: The monastery is renowned for its extensive collection of paintings, thangkas (religious scroll paintings), and intricate murals that depict stories from Buddhist scriptures.

Festivals, Events, and Rituals

Ralang Monastery is a vibrant hub of monastic activity, particularly during its annual festivals. These events are a spectacular display of cultural and religious traditions.

Pang Lhabsol

Pang Lhabsol is a unique and deeply significant festival celebrated in Sikkim, known for its powerful blend of religious devotion, cultural heritage, and a symbolic message of unity. The name "Pang Lhabsol" translates to "the worship of the witness god.

  • Guardian Deity: The festival is primarily celebrated to honor and pay homage to Mount Khangchendzonga, the third-highest mountain in the world. In Sikkimese tradition, the mountain is not just a geographical feature but a living deity believed to be the protector of the land and its people.
  • Symbol of Unity: Pang Lhabsol commemorates the historic alliance between the indigenous Lepcha and the migrating Bhutia communities, symbolizing the harmony and communal unity that defines Sikkim.
  • Royal Patronage: The festival, in its current form, was institutionalized by the third Chogyal (king) of Sikkim, Chador Namgyal, who is said to have choreographed the famous warrior dance after a divine vision.

Kagyed Chaam (Masked Dances)

Kagyed Chaam is a significant and vibrant masked dance festival celebrated in Sikkim, primarily by the Buddhist communities. It is a powerful spiritual and cultural event that symbolizes the triumph of good over evil and marks the transition from the old year to the new.

  • Symbolism: The term "Kagyed" is of Bhutia origin and refers to the "oral transmission of the eight tantric deities." The dance is performed to honor these deities and invoke their blessings to protect humanity from malevolent spirits, diseases, and calamities
  • Purification: The festival is essentially a rite of purification. It aims to destroy all the negative forces and obstacles that have accumulated during the year, paving the way for peace, prosperity, and good fortune in the coming one

Other Important Festivals

Saga Dawa

A major Buddhist festival celebrating the birth, enlightenment, and death of the Buddha. The monastery hosts special prayers and rituals.

Lhabab Duchen

Lhabab Duchen is a major Buddhist festival that commemorates the Buddha's descent from the heavenly realms. It's celebrated on the 22nd day of the ninth Tibetan lunar month, typically in October or November.

Mahakala Dance

This sacred dance is performed in the month of November and is a key part of the religious calendar. It honors the protector deity Mahakala

Visiting the Monastery

Timings & Entry

  • Timings: Ralang Monastery is generally open to visitors from 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM daily. Some sources mention opening hours as early as 6:00 AM
  • Entry Fee: There is no entry fee, though donations are welcome.

Location & Access

  • Location:Ralang Monastery is located in South Sikkim, near the town of Ravangla.
  • It is situated about 6 km from Ravangla and is nestled amidst lush greenery
  • Transport:
    • Nearest Airport: Bagdogra International Airport (IXB) in West Bengal
    • Nearest Railway Station: New Jalpaiguri (NJP) in West Bengal.

Best Time to Visit

  • Spring (March-May): The landscape comes alive with blooming rhododendrons and other flowers.
  • Autumn (September-November): This is considered by many to be the best time. The monsoon has just ended, leaving the air crisp and clean.
  • Winter (December-February): The weather is very cold, with temperatures often dropping to freezing point.

Tips for Visitors

  • Dress Code: Visitors are expected to dress modestly, with shoulders and knees covered.
  • Footwear: Shoes should be removed before entering the main prayer hall.
  • Etiquette: Maintain a quiet and respectful demeanor, and avoid loud conversations to honor the peaceful and spiritual environment.
  • Photography: Photography may be restricted in some areas - always ask for permission.
