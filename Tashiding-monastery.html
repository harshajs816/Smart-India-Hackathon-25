Tashiding Monastery

Tashiding Monastery, located in West Sikkim, is one of the most revered and sacred sites in the region. It belongs to the Nyingma order of Tibetan Buddhism and holds a special place in Sikkim's history and religious traditions. The name "Tashiding" means "The Devoted Central Glory."

History & Significance

Tashiding Monastery was founded in 1641 CE by Ngadak Sempa Chempo Phunshok Rigzing, one of the three monks credited with consecrating Sikkim's first king. The monastery is perched on a hilltop between the Rangit and Rathong rivers, with the majestic Mount Kanchenjunga in the background.

It is believed that the site was blessed by Guru Padmasambhava in the 8th century, who brought Buddhism to Tibet and the Himalayan region. This sacred blessing makes Tashiding one of the holiest sites in Sikkim.

The monastery is particularly famous for the Thongwa Rangdol chorten (stupa), which is believed to cleanse a person's sins just by the mere sight of it. The complex also has numerous other stupas and prayer flags, creating a deeply spiritual atmosphere.

Festivals, Events, and Rituals

The most famous and important festival at Tashiding Monastery is the Bhumchu (Bhum means pot, Chu means water) festival. This unique ceremony draws thousands of pilgrims from across Sikkim and neighboring countries.

The Bhumchu Ceremony

The festival is centered around a sacred vase filled with holy water, said to have been blessed by Guru Padmasambhava. Each year, on the 14th and 15th day of the first Tibetan lunar month (usually in February or March), the sealed vase is opened. Monks examine the water level to predict the fortunes of the coming year:

  • If the water level is high, it signifies a prosperous and peaceful year.
  • A low water level indicates famine or drought.
  • Muddy water suggests a year of conflict or disease.

After the prediction, a small amount of the holy water is distributed to devotees. The vase is then refilled with water from the Rathong River and sealed again for the next year.

Other Important Festivals

Saga Dawa

A major Buddhist festival celebrating the birth, enlightenment, and death of the Buddha. The monastery hosts special prayers and rituals.

Pang Lhabsol

While a Sikkim-wide festival, it is celebrated at the monastery to honor Mount Khangchendzonga, featuring the Pangtoed Chaam warrior dance.

Annual Cultural Calendar

Here is a structured cultural calendar for Tashiding Monastery, detailing the key events throughout the year:

January - March

Bhumchu Festival

Date: Varies (14th and 15th day of the first Tibetan lunar month)

Highlight: The opening of the sacred vase and the distribution of holy water to devotees.

April - June

Saga Dawa

Date: Varies (typically in May/June)

Highlight: A time of deep spiritual devotion and merit-making celebrating the Buddha's life.

July - September

Pang Lhabsol

Date: Varies (typically in August/September)

Highlight: The performance of the Pangtoed Chaam warrior dance honoring Mount Khangchendzonga.

October - December

Lhabab Duchen

Date: Varies (typically in October/November)

Highlight: The monastery is adorned with butter lamps and special prayers commemorating the Buddha's descent from heavenly realms.

Visiting the Monastery

Timings & Entry

  • Timings: The monastery is open to visitors daily from 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM.
  • Entry Fee: A small entry fee is charged.

Location & Access

  • Location: West Sikkim, perched on a hilltop between Rangit and Rathong rivers.
  • Distance: About 40 km from Gyalshing and 19 km from Yuksom.
  • Transport: Best reached by hiring a taxi from nearby towns like Pelling or Yuksom.

Best Time to Visit

  • Spring (March-May): Pleasant weather and clear mountain views.
  • Autumn (September-November): Crisp skies and panoramic vistas.
  • Winter (February-March): Ideal for witnessing the famous Bhumchu festival.

Tips for Visitors

  • Dress Code: Visitors are expected to dress modestly, with shoulders and knees covered.
  • Footwear: Shoes should be removed before entering the main prayer hall.
  • Atmosphere: The monastery is a serene place; visitors are advised to maintain silence and respect the spiritual environment.
  • Photography: Photography may be restricted in some areas - always ask for permission.
