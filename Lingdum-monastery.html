Lingdum Monastery

Lingdum Monastery, also known as Ranka Monastery or Pal Zurmang Kagyud Monastery, is a relatively modern but profoundly spiritual site located in the peaceful village of Ranka, about an hour's drive from Gangtok in East Sikkim. Completed in 1999, it stands as a testament to the preservation and continuation of Tibetan Buddhist traditions in India. The monastery is known for its stunning architecture, serene setting amidst forested hills, and its vibrant monastic community. It is the principal seat of the Zurmang Kagyu lineage of Tibetan Buddhism, under the spiritual guidance of the 12th Zurmang Gharwang Rinpoche"

History & Significance

Modern Foundation: The monastery was built in the 1990s and inaugurated in 1999. It was established by the 12th Zurmang Gharwang Rinpoche to serve as the new seat of the Zurmang Kagyu tradition, which originated in Tibet in the 1300s.

Architectural Beauty: Built in the traditional Tibetan style, the monastery complex is spread over a sprawling 21 acres. Its main prayer hall is a visual feast, featuring intricate murals, statues, and thangka paintings. A giant, five-meter-tall gilded statue of Buddha and a smaller statue of Guru Padmasambhava are the central attractions inside the hall

Training Center: Lingdum serves as a crucial training center for young monks, providing them with a space for religious studies and meditation. The sight of young, red-robed monks in the spacious courtyard is a common and charming sight for visitors

Festivals, Events, and Rituals

The monastery comes alive during various festivals, which offer a glimpse into the living traditions of the Zurmang Kagyu lineage.

Losar (Tibetan New Year)

Losar is the Tibetan New Year, the most important festival in the Tibetan Buddhist calendar. The festival marks the beginning of a new lunar year and is celebrated by Tibetans, Bhutanese, Sherpas, and other Himalayan communities. The word "Losar" is a combination of two Tibetan words: "Lo" (ལོ་) meaning year, and "Sar" (གསར་) meaning new. The festivities typically last for about 15 days, with the main celebrations taking place during the first three days. The festival is a time for family gatherings, rituals, and prayers for good fortune in the year to come

  • Prayer Flags: New prayer flags are hung to signify a fresh start and to spread good wishes and prayers on the wind.
  • Tsampa (Roasted Barley Flour): A staple food in Tibet, tsampa is used in various rituals, including the throwing of an offering called "chemar" as a gesture of respect and goodwill
  • Incense: The burning of fragrant incense is a common ritual throughout Losar to purify the air and make offerings to deities
  • Chaam Dance: This sacred masked dance, performed by monks, is a core part of the Losar celebrations. It is a form of moving meditation that tells stories from Buddhist scriptures and purifies the environment.

Gutor Chaam

Gutor Chaam is a sacred masked dance performed by monks of the Tibetan Buddhist tradition. It is a highly significant ritual that takes place on the 29th day of the last month of the Tibetan lunar calendar, which is typically a couple of days before the start of Losar (Tibetan New Year).

  • Masks and Costumes:The masks are a central element, transforming the monks into the deities they represent. The costumes are often bright and colorful, with intricate details
  • Movements: The dance is not merely a performance; it is a form of moving meditation. The slow, deliberate steps and powerful leaps symbolize the deities' power to control and subdue malevolent forces.
  • Music: The performance is accompanied by traditional Tibetan instruments, including cymbals, drums, and horns, which create a solemn and rhythmic sound that is integral to the ritual.

Other Important Festivals

Saga Dawa

A major Buddhist festival celebrating the birth, enlightenment, and death of the Buddha. The monastery hosts special prayers and rituals.

Lhabab Duchen

Lhabab Duchen is a major Buddhist festival that commemorates the Buddha's descent from the heavenly realms. It's celebrated on the 22nd day of the ninth Tibetan lunar month, typically in October or November.

Visiting the Monastery

Timings & Entry

  • Timings: The monastery is generally open to visitors from 6:00 AM to 6:00 PM or dawn to dusk.
  • Entry Fee: There is no entry fee, though donations are welcome.

Location & Access

  • Location: Lingdum is located in Ranka, about 20 km from Gangtok.
  • The journey by car takes approximately one hour
  • Transport: The road can be a bit bumpy, so it's advisable to hire a larger vehicle.

Best Time to Visit

  • Spring (March-May): This is an ideal time for nature lovers.
  • Autumn (September-November): Crystal-clear skies, stunning mountain views, perfect for outdoor activities
  • Winter (December-February): this is the best time to see snow-covered landscapes and experience a quiet, serene Sikkim

Tips for Visitors

  • Dress Code: Visitors are expected to dress modestly, with shoulders and knees covered.
  • Footwear: Shoes should be removed before entering the main prayer hall.
  • Atmosphere: The monastery is a serene place; visitors are advised to maintain silence and respect the spiritual environment.
  • Photography: Photography may be restricted in some areas - always ask for permission.
